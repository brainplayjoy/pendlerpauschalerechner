German Commuter Allowance 2026: The New Flat Rate Guide

Moving to a new country comes with many financial adjustments. If you are an expat working in Germany, understanding the local tax system can save you thousands of Euros. One of the most valuable tax reliefs available to employees is the Commuter Allowance, known locally as the Pendlerpauschale.

The year 2026 has brought a major simplification: the flat rate is now a uniform €0.38 per kilometre for the entire distance between home and work – no more complicated tiered calculations. This comprehensive guide breaks down exactly how the new system works, how it benefits you, and how you can claim your money back.

What is the Commuter Allowance (Pendlerpauschale)?

In Germany, the government allows you to deduct the cost of traveling between your home and your primary workplace from your taxable income. This deduction is designed to lower your overall income tax burden.

It does not matter whether you take the train, drive a car, ride a bicycle, or simply walk to work. If you commute, you qualify for this tax relief.

The allowance is not a direct payment from the government, but rather a tax deduction. This means it reduces your taxable income, which in turn reduces the amount of income tax you owe. For many employees, this can result in a tax refund of several hundred Euros each year.

The 2026 Flat Rate: €0.38 per Kilometre for All Distances

Starting in 2026, the German government has introduced a single, uniform flat rate of €0.38 per kilometre for the entire commute – regardless of distance. The old tiered system (e.g., €0.30 for the first 20 km, then €0.38) is no longer in effect. This change simplifies the calculation dramatically and ensures that all commuters benefit from the same rate.

For example, if you commute 30 kilometres one way, you simply multiply: 30 km × €0.38 = €11.40 per working day. Multiply that by the number of workdays in the year, and you get your total deductible amount.

Here's a quick breakdown of the new rule:

  • Flat rate: €0.38 per kilometre, no caps or tiers
  • Distance: The entire one‑way distance between home and work
  • No upper limit: You can claim every kilometre, no matter how long your commute

This change is particularly beneficial for long‑distance commuters, who previously received a lower rate after the first 20 km. Now, every kilometre is worth the same, making the calculation transparent and fair.

How to Claim the Allowance in Your Tax Return

To claim the commuter allowance, you simply need to fill out the relevant section of your annual tax return (Einkommensteuererklärung). You'll need to provide your home address, your workplace address, and the number of days you commuted. No receipts are required — the German tax office trusts your declaration.

If you use a tax preparation software or a tax advisor, they will automatically apply the new 2026 flat rate. Make sure to double‑check that the rate used is €0.38 per km for all kilometres.

Important things to remember when claiming:

  • Workdays only: Only count days you actually went to the office (home office days don't count)
  • Shortest route: You can claim the shortest practical route, even if you take a different way
  • Multiple jobs: If you have multiple jobs, you can only claim the commute to your primary workplace
  • Public transport: Even if you use public transport, you can still claim the per-kilometre rate

Who Qualifies for the Commuter Allowance?

Almost every employee in Germany qualifies for the commuter allowance, regardless of their profession or salary level. The only requirements are:

  • You are employed (not self-employed or a freelancer)
  • You have a regular workplace outside your home
  • The distance between your home and workplace is at least 1 kilometre

Even if you work part-time or are in a training program (Ausbildung), you can still claim the allowance. The only exception is if your employer fully reimburses your travel costs — in that case, you cannot claim the deduction as well.

For expats, it's worth noting that you qualify from your first day of employment in Germany, regardless of your nationality or visa status.

How to Calculate Your Total Deduction

The mathematical formula for the 2026 tax year is very straightforward:

One-Way Distance (km) × €0.38 × Number of Working Days = Total Tax Deduction

For example, if you commute 25 km to your office for 220 days, your calculation is: 25 × €0.38 × 220 = €2,090. Since this is below the €4,500 cap, you can deduct the full amount.

What About Working from Home (Home Office)?

If you work from home, you cannot claim the commuter allowance for those specific days because you did not travel. Instead, you can claim the Home Office Allowance (Homeoffice-Pauschale).

For every day you work entirely from home, you can claim a flat rate of €6.00, up to a maximum limit of €1,260 per year (which equals 210 working days).

Real-World Example: How Much Can You Save?

Let's look at a practical example to understand the real impact of the new 2026 flat rate.

Scenario: Anna works in Munich but lives 25 kilometres outside the city. She commutes 220 days per year.

  • Distance: 25 km one way
  • Daily deduction: 25 km × €0.38 = €9.50
  • Annual deduction: €9.50 × 220 days = €2,090

With a marginal tax rate of 35%, Anna would save approximately €732 in taxes. That's money back in her pocket every year!
