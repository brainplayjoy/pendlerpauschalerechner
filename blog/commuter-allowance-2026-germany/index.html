German Commuter Allowance 2026: The New Flat Rate Guide

Moving to a new country comes with many financial adjustments. If you are an expat working in Germany, understanding the local tax system can save you thousands of Euros. One of the most valuable tax reliefs available to employees is the Commuter Allowance, known locally as the Pendlerpauschale.

The year 2026 has brought major updates to how this allowance is calculated. The old, complicated tiered system is gone. This comprehensive guide breaks down exactly how the new system works, how it benefits you, and how you can claim your money back.

What is the Commuter Allowance (Pendlerpauschale)?

In Germany, the government allows you to deduct the cost of traveling between your home and your primary workplace from your taxable income. This deduction is designed to lower your overall income tax burden.

It does not matter whether you take the train, drive a car, ride a bicycle, or simply walk to work. If you commute, you qualify for this tax relief.

The allowance is not a direct payment from the government, but rather a tax deduction. This means it reduces your taxable income, which in turn reduces the amount of income tax you owe. For many employees, this can result in a tax refund of several hundred Euros each year.

The New 2026 Flat Rate: €0.38 per Kilometer

Starting in 2026, the German government has introduced a uniform flat rate of €0.38 per kilometer for the first 20 kilometers of your commute. For every kilometer beyond that, the rate increases to €0.45. This simplifies the previous tiered calculation and ensures higher reimbursements for long-distance commuters.

For example, if you commute 30 kilometers one way, you can claim: (20 km × €0.38) + (10 km × €0.45) = €7.60 + €4.50 = €12.10 per working day. Multiply that by the number of workdays in the year, and you get a significant deduction.

Here's a quick breakdown of the new rates:

  • First 20 km: €0.38 per kilometer
  • 21st kilometer and beyond: €0.45 per kilometer
  • Maximum deductible: No upper limit (unlike previous years)

This change is particularly beneficial for commuters with long distances, as the higher rate for the additional kilometers adds up quickly over the course of a year.

How to Claim the Allowance in Your Tax Return

To claim the commuter allowance, you simply need to fill out the relevant section of your annual tax return (Einkommensteuererklärung). You'll need to provide your home address, your workplace address, and the number of days you commuted. No receipts are required — the German tax office trusts your declaration.

If you use a tax preparation software or a tax advisor, they will automatically apply the new rates for 2026. Make sure to double‑check that the correct flat rate is used.

Important things to remember when claiming:

  • Workdays only: Only count days you actually went to the office (home office days don't count)
  • Shortest route: You can claim the shortest practical route, even if you take a different way
  • Multiple jobs: If you have multiple jobs, you can only claim the commute to your primary workplace
  • Public transport: Even if you use public transport, you can still claim the per-kilometer rate

Who Qualifies for the Commuter Allowance?

Almost every employee in Germany qualifies for the commuter allowance, regardless of their profession or salary level. The only requirements are:

  • You are employed (not self-employed or a freelancer)
  • You have a regular workplace outside your home
  • The distance between your home and workplace is at least 1 kilometer

Even if you work part-time or are in a training program (Ausbildung), you can still claim the allowance. The only exception is if your employer fully reimburses your travel costs — in that case, you cannot claim the deduction as well.

For expats, it's worth noting that you qualify from your first day of employment in Germany, regardless of your nationality or visa status.

Real-World Example: How Much Can You Save?

Let's look at a practical example to understand the real impact of the new 2026 flat rate.

Scenario: Anna works in Munich but lives 25 kilometers outside the city. She commutes 220 days per year.

  • Distance: 25 km one way
  • First 20 km: 20 × €0.38 = €7.60
  • Remaining 5 km: 5 × €0.45 = €2.25
  • Daily deduction: €7.60 + €2.25 = €9.85
  • Annual deduction: €9.85 × 220 days = €2,167

With a marginal tax rate of 35%, Anna would save approximately €758 in taxes. That's money back in her pocket every year!

For longer commutes, the savings are even more substantial. A 50 km commute with the same 220 workdays would result in an annual deduction of €3,850 and potential tax savings of over €1,300.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When claiming your commuter allowance, avoid these common pitfalls:

  • Forgetting to claim: Many expats don't know about this deduction and miss out on hundreds of Euros
  • Counting home office days: Only days you physically commute count
  • Using the wrong distance: Always use the shortest practical route
  • Not updating for 2026: The rates have changed — make sure you're using €0.38 and €0.45
  • Missing the deadline: Tax returns must be filed by the deadline (usually July 31st of the following year)

If you're unsure about any aspect of your tax return, consider using a tax software or consulting with a tax advisor (Steuerberater) who specializes in expat taxes.
