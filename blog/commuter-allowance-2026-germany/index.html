German Commuter Allowance 2026: The New Flat Rate Guide

Moving to a new country comes with many financial adjustments. If you are an expat working in Germany, understanding the local tax system can save you thousands of Euros. One of the most valuable tax reliefs available to employees is the Commuter Allowance, known locally as the Pendlerpauschale.

The year 2026 has brought major updates to how this allowance is calculated. The old, complicated tiered system is gone. This comprehensive guide breaks down exactly how the new system works, how it benefits you, and how you can claim your money back.

What is the Commuter Allowance (Pendlerpauschale)?

In Germany, the government allows you to deduct the cost of traveling between your home and your primary workplace from your taxable income. This deduction is designed to lower your overall income tax burden.

It does not matter whether you take the train, drive a car, ride a bicycle, or simply walk to work. If you commute, you qualify for this tax relief.

The Major 2026 Rule Change: A Flat Rate System

Before 2026, the commuter allowance used a tiered calculation. Commuters received €0.30 per kilometer for the first 20 kilometers, and €0.38 per kilometer starting only from the 21st kilometer.

As of 2026, this tiered system has been completely abolished. Germany now uses a simple, unified flat rate. You get a flat €0.38 per kilometer starting from the very first kilometer of your commute. This is excellent news for international professionals with shorter commutes.

Key Rules of the Calculation

To use a commuter allowance tool correctly, you must keep three fundamental rules in mind:

  • One-Way Distance Only: You can only claim the distance for a one-way trip, not the round trip.
  • Actual Working Days: You can only claim the allowance for days you physically traveled to your workplace.
  • The Shortest Route: The tax office (Finanzamt) generally expects you to calculate using the shortest regular road connection.

The Annual Maximum Limit and the Car Exception

The German tax office sets a maximum annual limit of €4,500 for the commuter allowance. However, there is a major exception to this rule based on your mode of transport.

If you commute using public transport, a bicycle, or as a passenger in a carpool, your annual deduction cannot exceed €4,500. If you use your own personal car or a company car, this €4,500 cap does not apply. Car drivers can claim the full calculated amount.

How to Calculate Your Total Deduction

The mathematical formula for the 2026 tax year is very straightforward:

One-Way Distance (km) × €0.38 × Number of Working Days = Total Tax Deduction

For example, if you commute 25 km to your office for 220 days, your calculation is: 25 × €0.38 × 220 = €2,090. Since this is below the €4,500 cap, you can deduct the full amount.

What About Working from Home (Home Office)?

If you work from home, you cannot claim the commuter allowance for those specific days because you did not travel. Instead, you can claim the Home Office Allowance (Homeoffice-Pauschale).

For every day you work entirely from home, you can claim a flat rate of €6.00, up to a maximum limit of €1,260 per year (which equals 210 working days).

How to Claim the Allowance on Your Tax Return

To get your money back, you must submit an annual German tax declaration (Steuererklärung). You can do this securely online using the official German government tax portal known as ELSTER , or through popular English tax software apps.

You will need to fill out Anlage N (the form for income from employment). You do not need to attach train tickets or gas receipts to your tax return initially, but you should keep them safe just in case the tax office asks for proof.
